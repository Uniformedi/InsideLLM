InsideLLM — Discovery Questions

Proactive buyer discovery — the questions to ask before, during, and after the demo to surface actual priorities, qualify fit, and earn the follow-up meeting. Separate from Q&A anticipation (which is reactive).

Audience Parent Organization portfolio leadership + Compliance Manager Drafted 2026-04-22 Use Thursday study; Friday field use
Discovery principle: the demo is one-to-many in the room but every individual has a different reason for being there. The economic buyer, the compliance officer, the technical evaluator, and the skeptic each need a different hook. Discovery questions reveal who is who and what each one actually cares about — so the demo flow can be tuned on the fly.

Phases

  1. 1. Pre-meeting research (before walking in)
  2. 2. Opening — first 2 minutes (before the demo proper)
  3. 3. In-demo pivot questions (mid-flow adjustments)
  4. 4. Post-demo consultative questions (last 10 minutes)
  5. 5. Disqualification triggers

1. Pre-meeting research — before walking in

What to know before you startdone 24–48h in advance

Walk in knowing more about them than they expect. Makes the opening crisp and signals seriousness.

About Parent Organization

  • Which of the 32 portfolio companies are in regulated industries?
  • Any recent regulatory enforcement actions or public compliance incidents across the portfolio?
  • Who is the current CTO / CIO / CISO equivalent at the Parent level?
  • Is there a central compliance or risk-management function, or is it per-company?
  • Any public commitments to AI adoption, governance, or responsible AI?

About attendees

  • Role and reporting line of each person confirmed for the meeting
  • LinkedIn backgrounds — especially compliance / tech attendees' prior experience
  • Any prior interactions (conference, email, mutual connection)?
  • Who is the champion who invited you? What's their motivation?
  • Who is likely to be the most skeptical voice in the room?

About the industry context

  • For each industry represented in the portfolio: one current regulatory development or enforcement trend to mention naturally
  • At least one named competitor / peer in each industry who is publicly using AI
  • Current AI-related litigation or CFPB / HHS OCR / state-AG action affecting that industry

2. Opening — first 2 minutes (before starting the demo)

Ask these, in this order, before demo step 190–120 sec

Fast, friendly, and pointed. Do not trade structure for rapport. Their answers shape which moments you emphasize during the demo.
"Before I dive in — what made this meeting worth your time today?"
their actual motivation, whether it's compliance anxiety, cost, a specific incident, or strategic positioning
whichever motivation surfaces, weight the matching demo step longer (compliance → step 6 guardrail source; cost → mention Ollama; strategic → portfolio view)
"In one sentence, where does AI already touch consumers in your portfolio today?"
scope and sophistication of current AI use; whether they're past the experimentation phase
if they're early: simplify the demo language; if they're advanced: use technical terms without hedging
"we have no current AI use" — proceed but lower the sales intensity; this is a strategy conversation, not a buying one
"Who in the room today is thinking hardest about regulatory risk?"
the compliance officer identifies themselves; you now know who to make eye contact with during the OPA deny moments
direct the Turn 2 OPA deny ("FDCPA §1692c(a)(1)") straight at them — it's the money moment for their role
"If this worked perfectly for the 32-company portfolio, what does that look like a year from now?"
whether their vision is compliance-defensive, cost-offensive, competitive-strategic, or all three
close the demo with a call-to-action that maps to their articulated vision

3. In-demo pivot questions — mid-flow adjustments

Use these when you notice the room drifting~10 sec each

Short, well-placed questions during the demo re-engage attention without derailing the script. Deploy when you see someone checking their phone.
"Does this portfolio view match how you think about governance today, or is it different?"
after step 1 (Portfolio View). Reveals whether your mental model of their org is right
"When you have a compliance question mid-flight right now, who answers it — the vendor or your team?"
after step 6 (Guardrail source). Sets up the "your GC can read this" moment
"If you replaced this dispute handler with your specific workflow, what would the first hard rule be?"
during step 3 (Dispute Handler). Shows you understand the extensibility; them thinking in their terms is buying behavior
"For this audit chain — is that the format your examiners want today, or different?"
after step 5 (Audit Trail). Surfaces specific regulator-format requirements you may need to adapt
"What's a governance incident that has cost you real money in the last 2 years?"
after step 6, before close. Reveals actual pain they've experienced — much more valuable than hypothetical concerns
if something concrete surfaces, translate it to a line item in the follow-up email as a specific capability they'd avoid that pain with

4. Post-demo consultative questions — last 10 minutes

After the 12-minute flow, before the meeting ends5–10 min

These are the questions that surface whether this is a real opportunity and what the real decision process looks like. Do not skip.
"Which part of what you saw most landed with you personally? And which part left you with questions?"
candid feedback + open objections. Pure gold for follow-up tailoring.
"If you were going to say yes to a first tenant deployment — which company in your portfolio would be the natural starting point and why?"
whether they're already thinking operationally about deployment; which vertical they see as the wedge
"we wouldn't do a first tenant" — this is a real signal that they're still in strategy mode, not procurement mode
"Walk me through how a decision like this gets made at Parent Organization. Who's involved, what does the process look like, and what's the typical timeline?"
decision-makers by name, stage gates, procurement cycles, fiscal-year timing
map every named stakeholder to a follow-up material targeted at them
"What do you compare us against? Is there a 'if not InsideLLM, then…' answer in your head?"
the real competitive alternative — often it's internal build, a hyperscaler tool they already have, or "do nothing"
if the alternative named is on the Competitors.html sheet, deploy the specific counter; if it's "internal build," quantify the hidden cost
"What would cause this not to happen?"
the real objection. Budget, timing, political opposition, competing priorities, someone's skepticism
address the stated blocker in the follow-up email within 24 hours; if they can't name a blocker, the conversation is politeness, not a deal
"Is there anything you didn't see today that you expected to?"
implicit requirements. "I expected to see integration with [X]" is a specific gap you can address
"Who else in your organization should we be talking to?"
warm referral. Even if they can't buy, they might introduce you
always ask for a specific intro ("would you forward the Executive Summary to [name] this week?")

5. Disqualification triggers

When to stop selling and start being honestas they happen

Selling into no-fit situations wastes their time and yours. The following are clear signals to pivot the conversation to a different relationship (design partner, referral source, long-term watcher).
"We require SOC 2 Type II certification before any vendor assessment."
You cannot serve them today. Offer: design-partner engagement now, with SOC 2 Type II targeted Q1 2027; or walk away gracefully with a quarterly check-in.
"Our procurement policy prohibits source-available licenses categorically."
If the Professional/Enterprise licensing addendum doesn't resolve this, there's no path. Don't oversell the addendum — if their policy is absolute, it's absolute.
"We need a vendor with at least N named customers."
You're not there yet. Offer: design-partner pricing in exchange for becoming one of the reference customers they would require from a future vendor.
"We're looking for a pure SaaS solution; we don't operate on-prem infrastructure."
You could offer Uniformedi-managed deployment on their private cloud — that's Enterprise-tier and meaningful. But if they want true multi-tenant SaaS, they're a bad fit.
"Our AI strategy is to standardize on [Microsoft / AWS / Google] across the portfolio."
Hard fit. Pivot to: "Could we be the governance layer that sits alongside [Copilot / Bedrock / Vertex] for workflows that need policy-as-code that those platforms don't natively provide?" If yes, different sales motion. If no, walk.
"We're exploring; we don't have a budget allocated for this."
Real but salvageable. Ask: "When would budget cycle planning happen for something like this?" Answer becomes the next touchpoint date.
